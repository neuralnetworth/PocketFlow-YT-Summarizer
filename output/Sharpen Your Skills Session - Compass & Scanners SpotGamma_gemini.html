Mastering the Compass: A Visual Guide to Finding High-Probability Trades

  • Rank vs. Percentile: Why does the Compass use IV Rank, and how does it change your analysis compared to traditional IV Percentile?
    The Compass tool deliberately uses a rank-based system instead of a percentile-based one to better identify and emphasize statistical outliers, which are critical for mean-reversion trading strategies.
    1. Defining the Terms:
      • IV Percentile measures the percentage of days in the past year that a stock's implied volatility (IV) was lower than its current level. It gives a broad sense of where IV is relative to its entire history.
      • IV Rank measures where the current IV is in relation to its highest and lowest points over the past year. A rank of 100 means IV is at its 52-week high; a rank of 0 means it's at its 52-week low.
    2. Why Rank is Used: The primary reason for choosing rank is its focus on extremes. The speaker notes, "IV rank is going to give you those outliers within the last year... we want those outliers to be where our anchors are because that's where we expect the strongest potential reversion back." Rank excels at flagging when volatility is at a tradable peak or trough.
    3. Key Trade-Offs:
      • For Mean-Reversion Traders: Rank is superior. It clearly signals when volatility is at an extreme high (a potential opportunity to sell premium) or extreme low (a potential opportunity to buy it), which are the core signals for these strategies.
      • For General Context: Percentile can be more nuanced. A stock could have an IV Rank of 50 (mid-range) but an IV Percentile of 80 if it has historically spent most of its time at very low volatility levels. Percentile provides a more distributed view, while rank focuses exclusively on the high-low range.
